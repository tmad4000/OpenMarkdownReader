01

What the product promises

Open arbitrary files.

.md, .markdown, .txt, and whatever else belongs in a folder you already own. No mandatory vault model. No rehoming notes just to read them.

Stay local-first.

The whole premise is that the filesystem is the product boundary. That keeps git, backup tools, and file watchers useful.

Auto-save can be the easy path.

Low-friction editing matters more than more chrome. The app is designed so the save loop can be near-invisible when you want it that way.

Obsidian-compatible where it counts.

Wikilinks fit naturally, so users get familiar connective tissue without inheriting a vault-only worldview.

02

A calm three-step workflow

1

Open a file or folder.

Browse a normal project tree or jump straight into a note from disk.

2

Read, edit, or switch modes.

Preview and edit sit close together, so moving between them does not feel like a context shift.

3

Keep moving.

Multi-tab work, sidebar navigation, and file-level persistence keep the note stack honest.

03

Positioning against vault-first apps

OpenMarkdownReader

Feels like a notebook app for people who already live in files.

The distinct message is not “we are another note graph.” It is “use your existing markdown, keep your filesystem workflow, and still get a polished reader/editor.”

Vault-first apps

Great when you want a managed world.

They are useful, but they often assume import, structure, or a new organizing model before you can get to the actual document.
